Reading Glasses

Reading Glasses

There comes a certain time in your life when you reach a point of not being able to read up close without stretching your arms to a particular limit, you may need to consider single-vision reading glasses for the ease of reading,

Reading glasses come in two main styles:

  1. Full frames, in which the entire lens is made in the reading prescription.
  2. "Half-eyes," which are the smaller glasses that sit lower down on your nose.

Full reading glasses are suitable for people who spend a great deal of time concentrating on material close-up. These give you a larger field of view for reading, but if you try to look up and across the room through them, everything appears blurry.

In contrast, "half-eye" reading glasses allow you to look down and through the lenses for near work, and up and over them to see in the distance.

Generally, people who have never needed glasses in the past will start out with a pair of reading glasses rather than progressive lenses or bifocals, which are usually a better choice if you have a need for distance as well as near correction.

Also, if your reading glasses are the type that force you to lean your head back in order to view your monitor, you're placing unnecessary strain on your neck muscles.

Computer users really should invest in prescription computer glasses for the greatest comfort and protection from high-energy visible blue light when viewing computer screens and other digital devices.

How To Care For Your Glasses

How To Care For Your Glasses

Here are some great tips on how to take proper care of your eyeglasses that will help make them last longer

Rinse

Always rinse your glasses with clean water before wiping or cleaning them. Even tiny particles of dust or dirt can settle on your lens, and wiping those around on a dry lens, can leave tiny scratches on the frame.

Spray Carefully

If you use a chemical, use sprays or cleansers that are specifically made to clean eyeglass lenses. Never use household cleaners, as these chemicals contain ammonia, which will actually tear off any coating that is on the lens.

Air Dry

It is always suggested to allow your glasses to air dry. This is another great way to keep any materials from getting on to your lens. If you can't set them down to air dry, wipe them down with a soft, clean, lint-free cloth.

Use the Right Cloth

NEVER use paper towels, tissue, or napkins to dry your lenses. All of these materials, regardless of how soft they are on your skin, have a textured surface and can easily scratch your lenses.

Also, refrain from using the tail of your shirt. If the clothing is not 100% cotton, the fibers in the fabric will scratch the lens of your eyeglasses over time. The clothing can also have dirt on it, which means the residue ends up on your lenses.

Grip Firmly

Hold your frames by gripping the piece that crosses the bridge of the nose. This will keep you from accidentally bending the frame while you clean. Bent glasses can negatively affect the way you see out of your glasses. Plus, if your frames are bent out of shape, they might feel uncomfortable.

Store Properly

Store your glasses when you're not wearing them. This isn't just a great way to keep dust and dirt away from your eyeglasses, but it also protects your specs from getting scratched, bent or broken. Microfiber pouches are also great to keep at your office desk or on your nightstand for glasses you don't necessarily wear all the time, like reading glasses.

Place Carefully

Don't lay your glasses lens down. This just leads to scratched lenses overtime.

Wash Often

Washing your glasses at least once a day will keep your lenses in their optimal state. The cleaner your glasses, the less your eyes have to strain to see through smudges, dirt, and dust.

Contact lenses: Do’s and Don’ts

Contact lenses: Do’s and Don’ts

DO keep your hands clean.

Your hands can be covered with germs, so wash them well before putting in or taking out your contacts. Make sure you use clear, lotion-free soap, and dry your hands thoroughly.

DO keep your lens case clean.

Bad hygiene practices are linked to a higher risk for contamination of the contact lens case. People who don’t clean and dry their contact cases, and wash their hands with soap and water before handling them, have a higher chance of having microorganisms in their cases. To properly clean your case pour all contact lens solution out of the case, rub it with a clean finger, then rinse it with fresh solution. Wipe it dry with a tissue, and store it upside down (caps, too) on a tissue until you're ready to remove your contacts at night.

DON’T “top off” contact lens solution.

Always use a fresh contact lens solution when you’re storing your contacts overnight. Adding a new solution to the old one in the case, or cleaning lenses with water has been linked to cases of Acanthamoeba keratitis, a rare but painful infection that is difficult to treat.

DON’T buy contacts without a prescription.

The eye’s surface has unique properties for each of us, so any contact lens, decorative or prescriptive, needs to be assessed by an ophthalmologist before use.

DON’T sleep without removing your contact lenses.

Sleeping with contact lenses increases the risk of an eye infection by approximately 10 times, so sleeping in them, even part-time, is harmful.

DON’T bathe with contacts in.

Avoid showering in contact lenses, and remove them before using a hot tub or swimming. “Water has small organisms that can lead to an eye infection, thus when it comes in contact with lenses the organisms increase in number and strength, ultimately leading to an eye infection.

Extra Pairs

Extra Pairs

For the road

The sun’s glare or bright headlights often are obstacles for people driving the vehicle. Eyewear with a special coating called AR or anti-reflective coating is a must for such times. The coating helps in cutting down the glare and reflections and increases contrast and sharpens your distant vision.

For the screens

Special computer glasses are recommended while working on a computer or a laptop. These glasses minimize the strain on your eyes that can happen due to long working hours. Eyeglasses with blue cut lenses minimize the amount of harmful blue light and help in ease of screen time for your eyes.

For sports-time

If you are a sports lover and cannot compromise on your performance, the best choice for you is to take care of your eyes so you can concentrate on your game. Sunglasses Frame Styles

SUNGLASSES FRAME STYLE

Polycarbonate

These impact-resistant lenses are a good choice if you play sports, work where your eyeglasses could easily get damaged, or have kids who are tough on their specs. They also have built-in UV protection.

Gradient Sunglasses

A gradient lens is darker on the top and lighter on the bottom. This design is ideal for reading outdoors, and/or leaving the sunglasses on for days that are not as bright. The density of the darkest part of the lens and the color can be varied to accommodate for each individual’s preference.

Photochromic Sunglasses

These adjust to the amount of light. When the lenses are exposed to UV rays, these grow darker to protect the eyes. Under dim light, they become clearer and brighter. Photochromic lenses make it more convenient for the user, since you don't have to change from sunglasses to glasses, depending on whether you are outdoors or indoors.

Polarized Sunglasses

These effectively eliminate glare and light reflections while allowing other light to come in. As a result, the user can see clearer colors, minus the glare. The polarized lenses can be added as a layer or coating to the lens. Polarized lenses will work for most outdoor situations, but is not advisable for icy conditions or when viewing LCD displays.

UV Protection Sunglasses

This prevents the sun's harmful rays from penetrating and provides complete UV protection and is applied to plastic and glass lenses.

Flash Sunglasses/Mirrored Sunglasses

This provides further protection in preventing the light to penetrate the lens. It does so by reflecting the light.

Trends Over The Years

Trends Over The Years

1900-OVALS

The early 1900s focused on minimalism in eyewear fashion, with small and oval frames being the most popular and commonly seen fashion statements.

1920-ROUND

The 20s were all about round frames all over, be it the sunglasses or eyewear frames. With no differentiation between the male and female styles, the round frames became extremely popular with the liberated female population. The round look was so wildly popular that it perpetuated through another decade and continued through the 1930s.

1940-BROW

The 40s brought a bit of sophistication in the eyewear fashion with the new “browline” trend that accentuated strong facial features. The browline became popular because of the combination of metal and plastic which became a hit with the consumers. With consumers searching for more diversification in their eyewear the inclusion with different colors, shapes, sizes, and materials with one thing in common, the “browline”.

1950-CAT EYE

Popularized in the 1950s by Marilyn Monroe and Audrey Hepburn, the cat-eye became a superhit in eyewear styles. The delicate touch of the cat-eye draws attention to the eyes and that made it popular with the female population. For extra flair, women even fancied a colorful rim or a dash of sparkle in the upper corners!

1960-GEOMETRIC SHAPES, OVERSIZED FRAMES AND TEA SHADES

Jackie O brought about the most notable and ground-breaking trend in eyewear of the ‘60s, with her dark oval sunglasses that quickly became the go-to look for fashion followers of the decade. As can be expected given the political and social climate of the 1960s, the decade's eyewear was equally wild and varied. Colorful geometric frames, as well as oversized bug-eye colorful styles, mirrored the uninhibited spirit of the decade's contemporaries.

1970-RECTANGULAR AVIATORS

While aviators were invented several decades before 1936, the square aviator with a double bridge became the defining eyewear style of the 1970s. Today, this masculine pair has also become popular with the female fashionistas, making an interesting and unique contrast to casual feminine day to day outfits.

1980-POPS OF COLOR

With the 80s came a new trend in eyewear – color! No more understated metals or tortoise neutrals, this decade was all about the oversized shapes in exaggerated colors. It gave the pop of color that any outfit needed to change the whole look.

1990-THIN OVAL

The 90's toned things down a bit – with smaller, more understated styles that were geared more towards function than fashion.

2000-RIMLESS

For the 2000s, the most popular trends went back to basics with this clean, simple look. Some chose to jazz it up with a colored lens!
